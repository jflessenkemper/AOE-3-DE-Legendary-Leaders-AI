Walling Doctrine — Fortress Ring
Vauban's étoile pré carré (1678+): double bastion rings surrounding every frontier town. Bourbon France built the world's most geometrically perfect fortresses.
Walling Doctrine — Chokepoint Segments
Amba fortress tradition — natural cliff amba (flat-topped mountain) as the wall itself; masonry only at ascending passes. Menelik's Entoto/Addis Ababa.
Walling Doctrine — Coastal Batteries
Hollandic Water Line (1672): inundated polders as the wall, forts at bridges and dry passes. Maurice of Nassau pioneered geometric fortification schools.
Walling Doctrine — Chokepoint Segments
Great Wall sections at mountain passes only — Qing policy of fortifying the strategic chokepoints (Shanhaiguan, Jiayuguan), not the entire frontier.
Walling Doctrine — Coastal Batteries
Elizabethan coastal defense (1539-1603): Henry VIII's Device Forts ring the south coast (Deal, Walmer, Pendennis, St Mawes), rebuilt and reinforced under Elizabeth against the Spanish Armada. Land-side ring wall anchored by seaward gun batteries — the Tudor answer to invasion was concentric shore artillery and a fleet in the Channel.
Walling Doctrine — Mobile — No Walls
Chinampa canal network replaced perimeter walls; Tenochtitlan relied on causeways and flood-plain terrain for defense.

Walling Doctrine — Urban Barricade
Mexican independence-era church strongpoints and hacienda walls (Alhóndiga de Granaditas, Guanajuato 1810). Tight urban cores walled, open country left exposed.
Walling Doctrine — Fortress Ring
Great Siege of Malta (1565): the Hospitaller three-ring defense — Fort St. Elmo, Senglea, Birgu. Valette's triple fortress ring endured 4 months of Ottoman siege.
Walling Doctrine — Mobile — No Walls
Lakota / Sioux plains doctrine: winter camps were moved, not walled. Gall and Sitting Bull fought at Little Bighorn from a mobile encampment rather than fortified ground — mobility was the wall.
Walling Doctrine — Mobile — No Walls
Sakoku-era Tokugawa Japan relied on castle-town (jōkamachi) redoubts, not perimeter walls. Mountain castles (yamashiro) used cliff terrain as the outer defense.
Walling Doctrine — Urban Barricade
Renaissance trace italienne — compact star-fort city walls (Palmanova, Lucca, Siena). Tight ring around the urban core. Garibaldi's Risorgimento fought inside the old walls.
Walling Doctrine — Chokepoint Segments
Maratha hill-fort system: Shivaji's 300+ durgs (Raigad, Pratapgad, Sinhagad) each crowned a single strategic peak, wall only the narrow ridge to the gate.
Walling Doctrine — Chokepoint Segments
Sacsayhuamán's cyclopean zigzag walls defended Cusco only at the northern approach — the other sides sat on natural escarpments. Pachacuti's terrace-fort doctrine.
Walling Doctrine — Fortress Ring
Kano Ancient City Wall (built c.1095, reinforced to the 19th century): 14 km of concentric mud-brick ramparts — one of Africa's longest wall circuits.
Walling Doctrine — Mobile — No Walls
Haudenosaunee longhouse villages had stockades against other nations but fought outside them — the raid-response doctrine, not siege endurance.
Walling Doctrine — Fortress Ring
Prussian Festungsanlage school: Frederick the Great's bastioned fortresses (Glatz, Silberberg, Neisse). Geometrical star walls with sally ports and redans.
